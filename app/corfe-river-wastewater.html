Linked data · one river, four points

A wastewater linkage the data doesn’t yet name

Two sampling points sit either side of the same sewage works on the same river, but nothing in the graph says so. The only thing tying CORFE RIVER D/S CORFE STW to CORFE CASTLE STW is four characters of free text — “D/S”, for downstream — buried in a label. This page makes the relationship explicit, plots the phosphorus each point carries, and shows why naming the link turns four unrelated time-series into a source-apportionment model for the stretch of the river.

The link, once declared, lets a machine reason: any phosphorus the downstream river carries that the STW effluent does not account for can be attributed to something else — diffuse environmental discharge, or the intermittent storm overflow at SW-50900130. Today that inference is impossible to make automatically, because the link is a string, not a triple.

The shapes tell the story before any statistics do. The STW effluent (squares) is a concentrated point source. The downstream river (circles) sits an order of magnitude lower, because the works’ discharge is diluted into a far larger river flow. Neither series means much on its own. Together, and with the link declared, they become two knowns in a phosphorus budget for the stretch of the river.

Why it matters

Naming the link makes the stretch computable

Phosphorus in the downstream river at SW-50900712 is the sum of everything upstream of it:

river load (SW-50900712)
   =  upstream baseline        (SW-50908888)
    + STW treated effluent     (SW-50900722)   ← explicitly permitted & monitored
    + storm overflow spills    (SW-50900130)   ← intermittent, unmonitored here
    + diffuse catchment runoff (unattributed environmental discharge)

The STW is the one term in that equation that is explicitly accounted for: it is permitted, and its effluent is sampled 52 times. So if you subtract the upstream baseline and the STW’s known contribution from what the river actually carries, the residual is everything nobody has claimed — diffuse environmental discharge from the catchment, plus whatever the storm overflow released during a spill. That residual is where the regulatory interest lives, and it is only computable if a machine can follow SW-50900712 back to the discharges above it. Today it cannot: the topology exists only in a human-readable label.

Problem 1 — no flow, no load

Everything plotted here is concentration (mg/l), not load (mass per day), and a budget only balances in load: load = concentration × flow. The STW’s effluent flow — the volume of treated sewage it discharges each day — is not in the water-quality archive at all, and neither is the river’s gauged flow at the downstream point. So although the effluent is ~8× more concentrated than the river, we cannot yet say how much phosphorus mass the works contributes, nor what share of the river’s load that is. The concentration series set the budget up; without the STW’s discharge volume they cannot solve it. Linking the sampling points is necessary but not sufficient — the STW’s flow record has to be joined in too.

Problem 2 — the monitoring gap is the tell

The two points that would close the budget — the upstream baseline and the storm overflow — have no phosphorus data. So the sewage works is the only quantified source on a river that plainly receives more than one. Anything the STW’s effluent doesn’t explain is, by elimination, coming from the environment or from the overflow. Making the linkage explicit doesn’t just enable the sum — it shows exactly which gauge is missing.

The fix

Make the relationship a triple, not a string

The demonstrator already models each point as a geo:Feature with a geometry. What it lacks is the hydrological topology between them. The label "CORFE RIVER D/S CORFE STW" encodes it in prose; the fix is to assert it directly, using the demonstrator’s own extension namespace (ns1: <http://example.com/water-regulation/>):

@prefix sp:   <http://environment.data.gov.uk/water-quality/sampling-point/> .
@prefix ns1:  <http://example.com/water-regulation/> .
@prefix skos: <http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#> .

sp:SW-50900712                              # Corfe River, downstream
    ns1:downstreamOf     sp:SW-50900722 ,    # … of the STW effluent
                         sp:SW-50900130 ;    # … and the storm overflow
    ns1:upstreamBaseline sp:SW-50908888 .    # the stretch’s reference condition

sp:SW-50908888  ns1:upstreamOf  sp:SW-50900722 , sp:SW-50900712 .

# minimal, standards-friendly fallback if you don't want new predicates yet:
sp:SW-50900712  skos:related  sp:SW-50900722 , sp:SW-50900130 , sp:SW-50908888 .

For a standards-based route, the same topology aligns with OGC HY_Features (hyf:HY_FlowPath / upstream–downstream relations) rather than a bespoke predicate — but the point stands either way: one triple replaces a fragile substring, and the stretch becomes queryable. A single SPARQL query could then return “every river point downstream of a discharge whose phosphorus exceeds its upstream baseline” — the exact question this page had to answer by hand.
